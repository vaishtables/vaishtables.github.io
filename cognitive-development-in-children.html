Literature

"Cognitive Development in Children"

Arguments

  • distinguishes between "the problem of development in general and the problem of learning" (p. 176)
    • development of knowledge is tied to embryogenesis
      • spontaneous, emergent, innate
    • development of learning is adaptive

Definitions

  • operation (Piaget):

    "To understand the development of knowledge, we must start with an idea which is central to-the idea of an operation. Knowledge is not a copy of reality. To know an object, to know an event, is not simply to look at it and make a inental copy or image of it. To know an object is to act on it. To know is to modify, to transform the object, and to understand the process of this transformation, and as a consequence to understand the way the object is constructed. An operation is thus the essence of knowledge; it is an interiorized action which modifies the object of knowledge...in addition, it is a reversible action; that is, it can take place in both directions, for instance, adding or subtracting, joining or separating...Above all, an operation is never isolated. It is always linked to other operations, and as a result it is always a part of a total structure."

(p. 176-177)

Theories

  • [[2025061024]]
    1. sensorimotor stage
      • 0-18 mos. (1.5 yrs.)
      • practical knowledge is developed, substructures which serve as precursors to representational knowledge
        • object permanence
        • practical space
        • temporal succession
    2. preoperational stage
      • representational thought is developed, substructures which serve as precursors to operations
        • symbolic function conservation is not yet developed, precluding reversible operations
    3. concrete operational stage
      • first operations are developed but are only conducted on objects - classification - ordering - number - spatial - temporal - elementary logic, mathematics, geometry, physics
  • formal operational stage - development of hypothetic-deuctive operations - may construct new operations - propositional logic - combinatorial structures - complicated group structures - builds on concrete operations - classification by successive inclusions
  • four factors facilitating development of knowledge (i.e., acquisition of structures)
    1. maturation
      • development of knowledge cannot be entirely attributed to the interior changes of the nervous system
      • the chronological ages at which the operational stages develop vary cross-culturally although the sequence is preservered
    2. experience
      • practice navigating the physical world is also not enough to explain development of knowledge
        • at 8yrs., a child begins to conserve
          • first exhibits conservation of substance, then weight, then volume [[2025060946]]
            • conservation of substance (amount) is exhibited although it cannot be learned from experience and despite lacking conservation of weight and volume
      • distinguishes two kinds of experience
      • practical (pertains to learning properties of objects)
      • logical-mathematical (pertains to learning properties of actions)
    3. social transmission
      1. linguistic or educational
        1. insufficient for provoking knowledge if a child lacks the capacity for symbolic function which is need to receive this information
    4. equilibration [[2025060905]]
      1. reflected by displaying compensation, a critical cognitive factor that enables operational reversibility
        1. understanding an object that is transformed in one direction is necessarily also transformed in the other direction
        2. resolution of inconsistencies, imbalance, dissonance; absence of confusion
  • on equilibration and information-processing theory:

    “Equilibration, as I understand it, is thus an active process. It is a process of self-regulation. I think that this self-regulation is a fundamental factor in development. I use this term in the sense in which it is used in cybernetics, that is, in the sense of processes with feedback and with feedforward, of processes which regulate theniselves by a progressive compensation of systems. ”

(p.181)

  • rejects the stimulus-response schema of cognition as the response precedes the stimulus
    • unless the subject possesses a structure that may receive and integrate the signal, an object cannot be a stimulus
      • follows from the view of development of knowledge as emergent
  • illustrates with example of colleague's study of children acquiring conservation of weight vs. transitivity of weight
    • succesfully reinforced the former as it can be learned through practical experience
    • failed to reinforce the latter as logical-mathematical experience cannot be transmitted in this fashion, unless the prequisite structures are available
    • thus, no correlation between experimenter reinforcement and subject knowledge of transitivity
      • the opposite for conservation
        • however conservation of number can be learned, that is, its acquisition can be accelerated after 4 yrs.
          • at 5-5.5 yrs. children can generalize for objects outside of perception if trained using their own actions via additive operations
            • capable of recursive reasoning
  • relationship between stimulus and response is not merely one of association but of, fundamentally, assimilation [[2025060951]]

    Assimilation is not the same as association. I shall define assimilation as the integration of any sort of reality into a structure, and it is this assimilation which seems to me to be fundamental in learning, and which seems to me to be the fundamental relation from the point of view of pedagogical or didactic applications.

(p. 185)

  • emphasizes that learning is an active process
  • draws connection to Hull's theories
    • transformation responses = operations
    • internal reinforcements = equilibration
